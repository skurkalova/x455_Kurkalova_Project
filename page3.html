Sketching and Supplies

Scribbles and Scavenging

What are considered clay supplies?

The answer is really ambiguous because there are no “set” tools. Anything could be a tool for polymer clay. Anything like toothbrushes, nail art brushes, dental tools, sandpaper, etc. Whatever would make an imprint on clay! There are specialized tools out there designed for “polymer clay” but some of them tend to be expensive and overpriced. I’ve found that most of the tools at home are good enough for my clay pieces. That being said, there’s nothing wrong with using “polymer clay” tools either; some of them are convenient. You just have to make sure that the tools you’re using are ONLY for polymer clay. Polymer clay tools can be divided into two categories, in my opinion, “easy-to-get” tools and “specialized” tools.

The “easy-to-get” tools are the tools you have available and/or the “unconventional” tools. Like I mentioned earlier, toothbrushes and sandpaper are really effective for a gritty texture. Nail art brushes, sticks, and dotting tools are really good for smaller details. Even toothpicks are great for pressing small details into the base of a design; for example, leaves or rosebuds around a flower. Even for baking, there are “easy-to-get” tools. I use ceramic tiles to bake my polymer clay, sometimes with a baking sheet or paper in between. Ceramic tiles - anything less than 5x5” - are cheap to get at the store and can survive in the oven. They’re just hot to the touch when they come out of the oven, so be sure to use gloves.

As for the specialized tools for polymer clay, I find that local businesses are the best way to go. A lot of polymer clay artists have 3D printers and also sell cutters/stencils and texture rollers for other artists. You can find almost any cutter/roller design and create your own spin on the design with it. For example, I love doing floral designs for my jewelry, but replicating the exact design multiple times by hand is really hard. So, instead, I use the help of a cutter that makes seven little petals at once and put them together by hand. It makes the process easier.

What is a good design vs. a bad one?

Most of what makes a design good or bad is subjective. Every polymer clay artist has their own style and design preferences. What I make and design might not appeal to some and that’s okay; there are lots of jewelry artists out there. However, size and structural integrity can make or break a polymer clay piece. Polymer clay is pretty durable, if baked correctly, but the thinner the piece, the more likely it is to break.

One thing that can lead to a bad polymer clay design is not cleaning your tools. Polymer clay can get stuck in the tiny corners of your tools and transfer. For example, red polymer clay easily stains fingers and surfaces. Handling white polymer clay after using the red one might not be the best idea unless you wash your hands or change your gloves. I’ve had moments where I thought I cleaned my rolled and then discovered blue specs as I’m conditioning the yellow clay. It’s not the end of the world when it happens, but it can get annoying.

Another thing that can lead to a bad polymer clay design is baking incorrectly. Nothing hurts worse when you’ve assembled the most beautiful piece, baked it in the oven, and taken out a swollen, burnt husk because you’ve left it in too long. Baking polymer clay is tricky since it is sensitive to temperature changes and length of time. It’s good practice to read the polymer clay packaging to know the temperature and length of time per ¼ inch of clay. Not all polymer clay brands are the same and they each have their own set of rules. Underbaked clay will snap and crumble, whereas overbaked clay will blacken and swell. I have experienced both.

Resin is also another monster when it comes to designing polymer clay jewelry. I have not used resin but it’s popular for adding a shiny finish to polymer clay. If you thought polymer clay was a hassle to use, resin is even more so. It’s toxic to the touch - gloves are essential and it’s generally recommended to wear a gas mask when using it.
